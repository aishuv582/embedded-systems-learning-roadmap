Day 116 of Electronics – SPI (Serial Peripheral Interface) Practice & Advanced Concepts

1. SPI Communication Recap

SPI is a synchronous serial communication protocol developed by Motorola for high-speed communication between a master and one or more slave devices.

SPI Signals
Signal	Full Form	Direction
MOSI	Master Out Slave In	Master → Slave
MISO	Master In Slave Out	Slave → Master
SCLK	Serial Clock	Master → Slave
SS/CS	Slave Select / Chip Select	Master → Slave
2. SPI Modes (Important Interview Topic)

SPI has 4 modes, determined by:

CPOL (Clock Polarity)
CPHA (Clock Phase)
Mode	CPOL	CPHA	Clock Idle
0	0	0	Low
1	0	1	Low
2	1	0	High
3	1	1	High
Remember
CPOL → Idle clock level
CPHA → Edge on which data is sampled
3. Full Duplex Communication

SPI supports full-duplex communication.

This means:

Master sends data.
Slave sends data.
Both happen simultaneously.

Example:

Master transmits:

10110011

Slave simultaneously transmits:

01001110

Both devices receive data at the same time.

4. Daisy Chain SPI

Instead of separate chip-select lines:

Master
   |
Slave1
   |
Slave2
   |
Slave3

Advantages:

Fewer GPIO pins.
Useful in LED drivers and shift registers.

Disadvantages:

Higher latency.
All devices must shift data.
5. Multi-Slave SPI
         Slave1
           |
Master ----CS1

         Slave2
           |
         CS2

         Slave3
           |
         CS3

All slaves share:

MOSI
MISO
SCLK

Each slave has its own CS line.

6. Advantages of SPI
Very high speed.
Full duplex.
Simple hardware.
Low protocol overhead.
No addressing required.
7. Disadvantages
More wires than I²C.
Requires one CS line per slave (unless daisy-chained).
No built-in acknowledgment.
No error detection.
8. SPI vs UART vs I²C
Feature	SPI	UART	I²C
Clock	Yes	No	Yes
Duplex	Full	Full	Half
Speed	High	Medium	Medium
Wires	4	2	2
Multi-device	Yes	Limited	Yes
Addressing	No	No	Yes
